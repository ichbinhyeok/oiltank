# Source Audit - 2026-04-13

## Purpose
This note locks the official source anchors that shape the buried-oil-tank product. These are not just citations. They determine what the site is allowed to claim, what route families are justified, and where monetization can honestly sit.

## Highest-value official anchors

### New Jersey DEP - Unregulated Heating Oil Tanks
- URL: `https://dep.nj.gov/srp/unregulated/`
- Why it matters:
  - confirms residential heating oil underground tanks sit inside a distinct `UHOT` workflow
  - makes clear that certified closure contractors matter
  - ties leaking cases to environmental reporting and remediation
  - explicitly supports real-estate transaction framing
- Product impact:
  - `NJ` must be a launch state
  - `NJ` needs dedicated routes for `UHOT`, `NFA`, `closure contractor`, and `reporting`

### New York DEC - Underground Heating Oil Tanks Homeowner Guide
- URL: `https://dec.ny.gov/environmental-protection/hazardous-substance-bulk-storage/underground-heating-oil-tanks-homeowner-guide`

  - gives directional removal-cost language
  - notes delegated local regulation in places like `Nassau`, `Suffolk`, and `Westchester`

  - `NY` can support route families around local authority differences

## Source hierarchy
1. State environmental or remediation guidance
2. State cleanup, reporting, or homeowner guides
3. Local delegated environmental authority guidance
4. State insurance or consumer guidance
5. Secondary market cost references
6. Vendor content only for discovery, never for core factual claims

## Claims the product can support
- a state may require or strongly favor certified closure contractors
- a missing record does not prove there is no tank
- a sweep or locate may be the honest first move before excavation
- a leak concern can turn into reporting and cleanup workflow, not just simple removal
- buyer-seller timing can matter as much as raw removal cost

## Claims the product should not make
- that a buried tank is definitely present without a sweep or evidence
- that removal is always mandatory
- that abandonment in place is always acceptable
- that a property is clean without appropriate closure or remediation documentation
- that the site can replace a licensed environmental professional, closure contractor, or attorney
