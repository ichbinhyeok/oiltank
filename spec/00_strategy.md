# 00 Strategy

## Positioning
Build a state-first buried or abandoned residential heating-oil-tank decision site for users who already have a trigger event and need the next step.

This is not:

- a generic heating-oil information site
- a broad environmental-risk blog
- a contractor directory pretending to be editorial

This is:

- a post-trigger decision engine
- a transaction and remediation workflow product
- a state-aware lead-routing layer

## Core wedge
The core wedge is:

- `buried oil tank + home sale`
- `oil tank sweep before buying`
- `abandoned oil tank records`
- `remove versus abandon in place`
- `leak or cleanup uncertainty after discovery`

## Phase 1 wedge
Phase 1 should be narrower than the full category.

The first wedge is:

- buyer or seller in a transaction
- suspected buried tank or missing records
- sweep-first and records-first decision

If the first release does not feel like a `home-sale tank-risk engine`, it is still too broad.

## Why this can work
- The trigger is real and painful.
- Official guidance exists, but it does not close the next action.
- The topic has enough state variance to justify a structured route system.
- The user often needs a paid professional within the same session:
  - tank locate specialist
  - closure contractor
  - environmental cleanup professional

## Why now
- The search surface is not dominated only by giant publishers.
- Real-estate and inspection timing create high-intent sessions.
- The Northeast concentration makes a narrow, high-value state cohort practical.
- The product can reuse the same decision-engine logic that made `radonverdict` promising:
  - trigger state
  - threshold or procedure
  - local variance
  - next paid action

## The strategic bet
The bet is not that `oil tank` is a huge market.

The bet is that this is a **small but highly monetizable, high-stakes search market** where:

- the right user is worth a lot
- the right page can convert with low traffic
- state-first SEO can build a durable moat

## The market shape
This is a `Northeast-first` market, not a broad fifty-state content machine.

That is a feature, not a bug.

- it narrows implementation
- it improves sponsor relevance
- it keeps the route system honest

## What must not happen
The product fails if it drifts into any of these:

1. broad `heating oil` education
2. generic `home environmental hazards`
3. unsupported cost promises
4. national contractor marketplace behavior
5. thin local pages with no process difference

## Persona council verdict

### Demand realist
- Approves the topic because the trigger state is real and expensive.
- Strongest demand sits around `before closing`, `records missing`, and `sweep first`.

### SERP realist
- Approves the topic because search results are mixed:
  - official state guidance
  - local specialist pages
  - a limited number of broad publisher pages
- Rejects any plan built around broad head terms.
- Says the first release should bias toward `buyer-seller` and `sweep-first` pages, not a full route matrix.

### Funnel operator
- Approves the topic because the first CTA can be scenario-specific:
  - locate first
  - removal quote first
  - cleanup specialist first

### Risk operator
- Approves with guardrails:
  - do not overclaim
  - do not diagnose tank presence
  - do not blur remediation certainty

### Judge
- Final decision: build now
- Confidence: `8.5 / 10`

## Initial strategic conclusion
The strongest product is not `Oil Tank Cost Checker`.

It is:

`State-first buried oil tank decision engine for buyers, sellers, and homeowners after suspicion, discovery, or missing records.`

## 90-day objective
Within 90 days, the site should prove three things:

1. it can index and earn impressions for state and trigger routes
2. users click scenario-matched CTAs
3. at least a few qualified tank-sweep, removal, or cleanup leads arrive without phone-heavy operations

## Success condition
Success is not page count.

Success is:

- a narrow high-intent route set
- clear next-action routing
- sponsor-ready lead quality

## Failure condition
Failure is:

- broad educational traffic with low action intent
- thin state pages
- generic cost content with no state process
- too many route families before one wedge proves traction
